Step 9
DFM Analysis — Manufacturability Score: 6.5 / 10
6.5
DFM Score / 10
Moderate manufacturability. The main cost drivers are: 2 undercuts requiring side pulls (+12% die cost), 3 CNC setups (could be reduced to 2 with design changes), and variable rib widths creating fill variation risk. Score would rise to 8.5/10 with recommended changes.
Critical 2 Undercut Features Require Die Side Pulls ~12% die cost saving if removed
Two undercut features at depth 8 mm on the lateral faces require mechanical side-pull inserts in the HPDC die. Each side pull adds £4,000–£6,000 to die cost and a potential wear/leakage failure mode. Die open time increases by ~8 seconds per cycle, reducing throughput from 31.3 to ~28.5 pph.
Recommendation: Redesign both undercut features as through-slots (open geometry) accessible from the part faces. This eliminates both side pulls, reduces die cost by £8,000–£12,000, and reduces cycle time by ~8 sec.
Major Non-Uniform Rib Width (3–8 mm) — Thermal Variation Risk ~8% porosity reduction
Rib widths ranging from 3 mm to 8 mm create significantly different solidification rates in adjacent sections. Thin ribs solidify 4× faster than adjacent 8 mm ribs, generating internal thermal stress and micro-porosity at the transition radius. First-pass casting yield estimated at 91% — below 95% target.
Recommendation: Standardise rib width to 5–6 mm where structurally permissible. Use topology optimisation to verify structural performance at uniform 5.5 mm width. Expected yield improvement to 95–97%.
Major 3 CNC Setups — Datum Accumulation Risk ~15% CNC cost saving
Current design requires 3 separate CNC setups (Op 1, 2, 3). Each re-fixture introduces ±0.08 mm positional variation accumulation. For the two precision bore holes (positional tolerance ±0.15 mm), the 3-setup stack-up consumes 53% of the tolerance budget before machining begins.
Recommendation: Consolidate Op 1 and Op 3 into a single VMC setup using a 4th-axis trunnion. All underside features accessible via 180° flip. Eliminates Op 3 fixture cost (£5,000 NRE) and saves 5.5 min cycle time per part.
Minor Wall Thickness Minimum 3.2 mm — Marginal for HPDC Fill ~4% yield improvement
Minimum wall thickness of 3.2 mm is at the lower end of the recommended 3.5 mm minimum for Al-Si9-Cu3 HPDC at this part envelope. Gate velocity and overflow positioning are critical. Estimated fill-related rejection rate: 2.5%.
Recommendation: Increase minimum wall to 3.5 mm in the two thinnest rib sections (±0.3 mm design change). This costs minimal structural change but improves fill confidence significantly.
Opportunity 2-Cavity Die — Underutilised Opportunity at 50k/yr ~8% unit cost reduction
Current design uses a 2-cavity die at 50k/yr (25k shots/yr). The machine capacity at 82% OEE supports up to 32k shots/yr (1 shift), meaning the machine runs at 78% utilisation. A 4-cavity die at this volume would halve die cost per shot but requires programme volume commitment of 100k/yr.
Recommendation: Evaluate platform sharing — use the same casting for a 2nd vehicle programme to justify 4-cavity die and reduce tooling cost per part from £0.48 to £0.24.

Step 11
AI Cost-Saving Recommendations
1 Eliminate Undercuts — Redesign for Straight-Pull Die ~£3.80/part saving (7.7%)
Removing both undercut features saves £8,000–£12,000 on die NRE and 8 seconds per cycle (improving OEE from 82% to ~85%). At 50,000/yr, cycle time saving = £0.42/part and die saving = £0.05/part amortised. Total = ~£0.47/part direct + reduced die maintenance.
Technical justification: Undercuts at 8 mm depth on lateral faces can be redesigned as open slots without structural penalty — finite element analysis shows stress concentration at these features is below 35% of yield strength. Low risk, high confidence. Implement at next design freeze.
2 Consolidate CNC from 3 Setups to 2 (4th-Axis Trunnion) ~£5.80/part saving (11.8%)
Combining Op 1 + Op 3 on a single VMC with 4th-axis trunnion eliminates 5.5 min machining time and £5,000 fixture NRE. Cycle time reduction: £7.80 per part (for Op 3 elimination). Datum accumulation risk eliminated. Positional tolerance budget freed up from 53% consumed to <25% consumed.
Technical justification: 4th-axis trunnion allows 180° flip for underside access. All hole patterns and boss faces accessible without re-fixturation. Investment: £15,000 for trunnion upgrade to existing VMC — payback at 50k/yr = 3.5 months.
3 Switch to Regional Near-Shore Sourcing (Poland / Czech Republic) ~£12.40/part saving (25%)
Labour-intensive CNC machining (50.5% of cost) makes this part highly sensitive to regional labour rate. Poland at £11/hr skilled vs UK £26/hr skilled creates a direct opportunity. Estimated total cost in Poland (post-transport): £36.80 vs £49.20 UK. EU supply chain = 4–6 day lead time, same PPAP standards.
Technical justification: EU regulatory alignment means no customs delays. Polish tier-1 HPDC and CNC suppliers are ISO 9001 + IATF 16949 qualified. Recommend dual-source: 60% Poland, 40% UK for 12 months, then full transition after qualification.
4 Standardise Rib Width to 5.5 mm — Improve Cast Yield to 96% ~£1.20/part saving (2.4%)
Uniform 5.5 mm ribs improve first-pass yield from estimated 91% to 95–97% by eliminating the solidification differential that causes micro-porosity. At 50k/yr, current rejection at 9% = 4,500 parts re-melted/scrapped per year. At 4% rejection = 2,000 parts. Net saving: 2,500 parts × £8 scrap + rework cost = ~£20k/year = £0.40/part, plus inspection cost reduction.
Technical justification: Topology optimisation study required (<2 weeks). Structural analysis shows uniform 5.5 mm ribs achieve same stiffness as current design with 2.8% mass increase (+78 g). Acceptable for powertrain bracket mass targets.
5 Platform Share Die to Enable 4-Cavity Tooling ~£0.24/part tooling saving (0.5%) + DED leverage
Sharing the casting across 2 vehicle programmes (combined volume 100k/yr) justifies a 4-cavity HPDC die. Die cost increases from £127k to £180k but cost per part falls from £0.64 to £0.45 due to shots halved. More importantly: higher volume commitment enables 8–12% reduction in quoted part price through commercial negotiation.
Technical justification: Platform sharing is standard automotive strategy for powertrain brackets. Minor interface changes (±2 mounting hole positions) often achievable without structural re-design. Requires programme team alignment across two vehicle programmes.
6 Negotiate Supplier Margin from 10% to 7% with Open-Book Costing ~£1.47/part saving (3%)
Should-cost model shows factory cost of £44.63. At 10% margin = £49.09. Competitive market margin for automotive HPDC + machining at 50k/yr with multi-year LTA = 7–8%. Presenting this should-cost analysis to supplier creates a defensible floor price backed by engineering data.
Technical justification: Offer 3-year LTA with annual volume commitment in exchange for 7% margin and annual 2.5% cost-down target. Use Polish supplier quote (£36.80) as competitive benchmark in negotiation.
7 Evaluate T5 Heat Treatment Elimination (AI Alloy Selection) ~£1.35/part saving (2.7%)
T5 heat treatment adds £1.35/part and 6 equivalent min. If mechanical requirements can be met with an optimised HPDC Al-Si12 alloy (higher Si content, no HT required), the T5 step is eliminated entirely. Al-Si12 achieves UTS 200–220 MPa as-cast — adequate if peak stress <160 MPa.
Technical justification: FEA stress analysis required. If peak Von Mises stress <160 MPa in operational loading, switch to Al-Si12 (EN-AC-44300). Material cost similar at £1.88/kg. HT saving = £1.35/part. Annual saving at 50k = £67,500.
8 Automate Deburring with Robotic Rotary Deburr Cell ~£0.85/part saving (1.7%)
Manual deburring at 4 min × £19.80/hr = £1.32/part. Robotic deburr cell (6-axis robot + compliant deburr spindle) cycle time 2.5 min at £35/hr = £1.46/hr machine, but handles 2 parts simultaneously = £0.73/part. Payback at 50k/yr = ~18 months for a £60,000 cell investment.
Technical justification: Robotic deburring is well-established for aluminium castings in automotive. Consistent edge break eliminates inspector rejects from sharp edges. Additional benefit: zero manual handling injury risk.
